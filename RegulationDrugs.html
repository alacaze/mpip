Learning Objectives

  • Be aware of key federal and state laws regarding medicines
  • Be able to describe what is meant by a drug schedule
  • Be able to identify key responsibilities of medical practitioners in relation to drugs and poisons
  • Be able to critically reflect on your practice and the practice of others in relation to drugs and poisons

Key Concepts

  • Medicines are tightly regulated. Two important legislative instruments are the Standard for Uniform Scheduling for Medicines and Poisons (SUSMP) and Health (Drugs and Poisons) Regulation 1996 (HDPR)
    • Drugs are regulated according to schedules. The SUSMP provides the schedules for drugs in Australia.
    • HDPR stipulates the who, how, when and where of prescribing, dispensing and administration of drugs in Queensland
  • Professional decisions rely on considerations of the law, ethics and the clinical context—it is rare that a professional decision can be made relying exclusively on any one of these

Professional decision-making

Professional decisions

  • Professional decisions regarding screening, diagnosis and treatment take into consideration a wide array of factors
    • What is the evidence?
    • What does the patient want?
    • What is available to you?
    • What are you legally permitted to do?
    • What are you competent to do?
  • These decisions have important consequences and always have an ethical component

Medical practice and regulation

Being a health professional means practising in accordance with the law.

Practising outside of the law puts the public at risk.

If that is not a good enough reason; practising outside the law puts you at risk.

Risk of civil litigation

Risk of professional censure: unprofessional conduct/professional misconduct (perhaps leading to removal of registration).

Risk of prosecution in the courts for breaches of the HDPR

Law and medicines

A lot of legislation is relevant to medical practice. We are focusing on those related to medicines.

  • Medicines and Poisons Act 2019
  • Standard for Uniform Scheduling for Medicines and Poisons (referred to as: SUSMP)
  • Health (Drugs and Poisons) Regulation 1996 (referred to as: HDPR).

The nice thing about legislation is that it is written down.

New regulations are coming!

The Medicines and Poisons Act 2019 replaces the Health Act 1937 and provides a framework for replacing the Health (Drugs and Poisons) Regulation 1996.

The new regulations will be the Medicines and Poisons Regulation (Medicines Regulation). As at March 2021, this regulation is yet to be in force and the time frame for its implementation is unclear.

While there will be changes, there will still be much that is similar to the HDPR.

See here for discussion of the new regulation.

Medicines regulation: overview

Instrument Acronym Function
Standard for Uniform Scheduling for Medicines and Poison SUSMP Provides scheduling for all medicines available in Australia
Health (Drugs and Poisons) Regulation 1996 HDPR Provides regulations for prescribing, dispensing and supply of medicines in Queensland
Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme PBS The federal scheme that subsidizes medication costs

Scheduling (SUSMP)

  • Medicine scheduling is provided by the Standard for the Uniform Scheduling of Medicines and Poisons (SUSMP)
  • Roughly speaking, the more dangerous the drug—due to toxicity or potential for abuse—the more restricted the scheduling.
  • The regulations provided in the HDPR depend on the schedule of the medicine

Key Schedules

Different terminology used for drug schedules. The SUSMP has schedule 1–9. S2–4 and S8 are the schedules that you most frequently come across in pharmacy.
SUSMP HDPR Alternatives Examples
S2,S3 Poisons Over-the-counter drugs Paracetamol
S4 Restricted drug Prescription drug Antibiotics
S8 Controlled drug Dangerous drug Opioids

Prescribing, dispensing & administering medicines (HDPR)

  • The HDPR is organised by drug schedule: many regulations are specific to the schedule of the drug
  • Regulations are provided for controlled drugs (S8), restricted drugs (S4) and poisons (S2,S3)
  • Additional regulations are placed on regulated controlled and restricted drugs

Endorsements outline what activities each health professional is authorised to perform

Doctors

To the extent necessary to practice medicine, doctors are authorised to obtain, possess, prescribe, dispense, administer and supply controlled drugs (s58) and restricted drugs (s161)

Other health professionals: nurses, pharmacists, podiatrists, dentists, …, are authorised to perform subsets of these activities.

Regulated restricted and controlled drugs

  • Some drugs are subject to further regulations: regulated controlled drugs, regulated restricted drugs
  • Typically this is limiting prescription of these drugs to particular medical specialties or indications

Examples include dexamphetamine (s78), medical cannabis (s78A), isotretinoin (s186), …

Requirements on a prescription

  • The HDPR stipulates what needs to be on a legal prescription for controlled drugs (s79) and restricted drugs (s190)

Some of the additional items needed on a controlled drug prescription: single drug, patient’s date of birth, quantity in words in figures

Further restrictions on controlled drugs

  • Health professionals who are authorised to possess a controlled drug can’t self-administer it (s123)
    • Unless the controlled has been prescribed (appropriately) by someone else (s123(2))

There are similar restrictions in the Medicines and Poisons Act 2019—see s40. The wording is slightly different: “high-risk medicine” cf controlled drug

Additional guidance is provided in the Code of Conduct (9.2.2)

The guidance provided in the Medical Board of Australia Code of Conduct (9.2.2):

Good medical practice involves: …
Seeking independent, objective advice when you need medical care, and being aware of the risks of self-diagnosis and self-treatment

AMA Code of Ethics (2004, 2006, 2016) (2.1.16):

Avoid providing care to anyone with whom you have a close personal relationship, where possible

Cases & questions

A general model for decision-making

  1. Identify the problem and possible consequences of the problem
  2. Identify relevant law and professional standards that apply to the case
  3. Identify the available options for resolving the problem and the reasons for or against each one
  4. Formulate a plan of action to resolve the problem

A more general structure can be found in Winch et al. (2014)

Case outcome

This example is based on one of the cases discussed in Forbes (2014).

The patient in this case died from drug misadventure with toxic levels of the anxiety and pain medications.

She was identified as a patient at risk of drug misadventure by multiple prescribers and her pharmacist, but there was limited discussion between them and both continued to prescribe and dispense.

Further details

Drug Class Schedule Law/Guidance
Tramadol Opioid S4 HDPR, Medicines and Poisons Act 2019, Code of conduct
Ketamine NMDA antagonist S8 HDPR, Medicines and Poisons Act 2019, Code of conduct
Hydroxychloroquine Antiprotozoal, DMARD S4 HDPR, Code of conduct

You should have enough details from within the lecture to determine whether a medical practitioner can and should self-prescribe/prescribe these medicines in the specified context.

Hydroxychloroquine is used in some patients for rheumatoid athritis as Disease Modifying Antirheumatic Drug (DMARD).

It has also been discussed a lot as a possible treatment for COVID-19. At the start of the pandemic a lot of people presented to pharmacies with prescriptions for it—including medical practitioners, dentists and nurse practitioners. See here and Coombes et al. (2020).

There are a host of problems here:

  • Off-label use risked supply-chain problems for regular patients and for investigational trials to assess its efficacy in COVID-19
  • The hope that hydroxycholoroquine would be effective turned out false. Gould & Norris (2021) is well worth reading. The significant interest, mixed messages, quick publications and retractions and politicisation of uncertainty regarding hydroxychloroquine is a humbling case study for anyone interested in medical evidence and public policy
